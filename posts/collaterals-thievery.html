Of all the pathophysiologic phenomena that I have come across in my medical training, the one that most readily continues to astound me is the human body's capacity for decompression.

The medical lexicon is saturated with terminology reflecting the abundance of pilfering we perceive in our biological workings. Aberrant blood cells are sequestered by the spleen; narrowed arteries steal flow from adjacent circulations; solid tumors co-opt and recruit resident vessels and cells to fulfill their own agendas. It seems both natural and peculiar that it is under this worldview that such processes are named. Medical systems and their tendencies of classification have a long history of drawing borders between the normal and the abnormal, the physiological and the pathological, the acceptable and the condemnable. Attaching words with criminal connotations in the western, macro human context creates a semantic justification for intervening on the processes they describe and sanction harms that may be inflicted to successfully carry out such interventions.

The criminal framing of the bodies ways of taking and repurposing is further complicated when considering another term used in conjunction and explanation of these phenomena: compensation. By definition, the word suggests passive receipt of something by an entity, in particular to recompense for an undesireable or unwelcome state of affairs.

The unrelenting yet humble approach that radiology takes to bring this genre of thievery into view is one of the major principles that repeatedly draw me to the field. The radiologist understands that what is visible is just that: an image that can be imagined and, supposedly, perceived.
